Excited about dinosaurs? Look no further!

Our comprehensive guide covers most popular types of dinosaurs.



Triceratops

tri-SERRA-tops Name meaning:'three-horned face'

Triceratops was a plant-eater with specialised teeth for cutting and slicing and a huge stomach for digesting tough plant matter. It would have used its horns for defending itself from predators like Tyrannosaurus.

Stegosaurus

STEG-oh-SORE-us Name meaning:'roof lizard'

This slow-moving plant-eater used spikes on its tail to fend off would-be predators. The jury is still out on what the spiny plates on its back were used for.

Tyrannosaurus

tie-RAN-oh-sore-us Name meaning:'tyrant lizard'

An infamous meat-eating predator. Look at the evidence as to whether Tyrannosaurus hunted in packs or alone.

Diplodocus

DIP-low DOCK-us Name meaning:'double beam'

The Museum's cast of a Diplodocus skeleton is on a tour around the UK. What did this giant look like and how did it hold its enormous neck?

Coelophysis

seel-OH-fie-sis Name meaning:'hollow form'

This small meat-eater was one of the earliest dinosaurs. It was fast and agile and would have fed on animals like small reptiles and insects.

Achillobator

a-kil-oh-bah-tor Name meaning: 'Achilles hero' 5.0m-long large theropod.

Barapasaurus

Barapasaurus was a primitive but very large sauropod.

Centrosaurus

Centrosaurus had one horn on its snout and spines around the back of its neck frill.

Dromiceiomimus

  • Diet: omnivorous omnivorous food icon
  • Country:Canada
  • Period:Late Cretaceous, 74-70 million years ago
  • Teeth:horny beak
  • How it moved:on 2 legs
  • Food: plants, possibly insects and animals too Dromiceiomimus looked very like a modern ostrich, but without the thick layer of feathers (it may have had some feathers).
Dromiceiomimus is now a nomen dubium (dubious name), because the fossils are no longer thought to represent a distinct dinosaur type.

Erketu

Erketu is known from articulated cervical vertebrae (backbone), a sternal plate (breastbone) and an articulated lower leg.
